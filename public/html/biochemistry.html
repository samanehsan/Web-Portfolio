Development of Small Molecule Inhibitors Targeting the Transcription Factor ERG

At the University of Virginia School of Medicine, I am developing small-molecule inhibitors for the transcription factor ERG, a driver of disease in several cancers. Transcription factors have traditionally been considered "undrug-able", however recent studies have revealed a mechanism of auto-inhibition in the Ets-family of transcription factors, of which ERG is a member. Currently, I am screening chemical derivatives of identified hits using fluorescence polarization in order to optimize potency. In addition, I am investigating characteristics of the drug target, such as its recently identified mechanism of auto-inhibition, to provide further insight into its function and regulation.

Investigation of a Putative Phosphoserine Aminotransferase from Cytophaga Hutchinsonii

As part of the biochemistry lab course for undergraduate majors, I am working in a team of students to investigate the function of a putative phosphoserine aminotransferase from Cytophaga hutchinsonii. We conducted bioinformatics analysis and kinetic assays to determine substrate specificty and the kinetic parameters of those substrates. The collaborative nature of the course allows members to leverage their strengths in order to drive progress.
